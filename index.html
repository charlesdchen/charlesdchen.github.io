Doctoral research (2018-2023)

During my graduate studies at Washington University in St. Louis, I co-founded the PAINT committee and established a pipeline to transfer postmortem brain tissue and coordinate imaging-pathology studies between the Imaging and Neuropathology Cores of the Knight ADRC. This increased cooperation between the two cores allowed me to start a study comparing diffusion MRI cellularity predictions with cell densities derived from histology. I was also able to publish studies comparing amyloid-β and tau PET predictions with amyloid-β plaque and tau tangle densities derived from histology.

I also developed an algorithmic approach for finding optimal regions of interest and task-based cutoffs for Alzheimer disease biomarkers. I validated my approach by comparing my tau PET cutoff for biomarker positivity with visual reads as performed by a pair of radiologists. My algorithms along with their resultant ROIs and cutoffs are now being used in several studies at the Knight ADRC.

Post-baccalaureate research (2015-2018)

In college I wanted to study brain function, but never got the chance. After college, I had the opportunity to study brain function in the context of learning risks and rewards in an uncertain environment. I thought that studying how biological neurons encoded risks and rewards could inform the design of artificial neural networks for reinforcement learning. At the time, there was a breakthrough in using neural networks to learn how to play Atari games without human supervision by using "only the pixels and the game score as inputs". However, when watching videos of how the neural network learned to play, I noticed it failed to score any points on the game "Montezuma's Revenge" and thus failed to learn anything at all.

I thought that one way to remedy this was to add a "reward" signal for exploring the game environment to plan for the future instead of relying on the final game score as the sole reward. I ended up running experiments to measure a monkey's neuronal activity while it watched visual cues associated with different probabilities of future rewards. Interestingly, there were distinct neuronal signals associated with anticipating an uncertain reward (pre-reward ramping) and receiving a reward with a low probability of happening (post-reward spiking).

Undergraduate research (2011-2015)

In college I worked on developing a way to estimate axonal fiber directions and delineate cortical surfaces from brain tissue sections. I was given brain tissue, a microscope, and a stack of paper, and was asked to draw what I saw. This took a lot of time.

I decided to digitize the tissue sections so I wouldn't have to spend so much time drawing everything I saw under the microscope. Then I manually annotated everything using Adobe Illustrator. This still took a lot of time, so I wrote a MATLAB script to estimate axonal fiber directions using structure tensors and delineate cortical surfaces using B-splines.

When comparing these microscopy estimates with diffusion MRI estimates of axonal fiber directions and cortical surfaces, I confirmed that microscopy and diffusion MRI showed the greatest agreement in axonal fiber direction along the gyral crowns but the greatest discrepancy along the sulcal banks and fundi of the cortical surface. This work supported the idea that there was a "gyral bias" in diffusion MRI tractography and motivated the development of new tractography algorithms.
