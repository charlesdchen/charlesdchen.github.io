Amyloid-β

Pittsburgh compound B (PiB) is a radiotracer for positron emission tomography (PET) imaging that binds to amyloid-β in the brain. However, the relative contributions of diffuse versus cored/compact amyloid-β plaques to the PET signal is incompletely understood, especially in the rare, dominantly inherited form of Alzheimer disease (DIAD). Here we compared in vivo PiB PET with follow-up microscopy of amyloid-β burden in individuals with typical late-onset AD and those with DIAD. We found that, while the PiB PET signal predominantly reflects diffuse amyloid-β plaque burden in DIAD, it correlates with both diffuse and cored/compact amyloid-β plaque burdens in typical AD.

Tau

Flortaucipir is a radiotracer for positron emission tomography (PET) imaging that binds to tau in the brain. However, the relative contributions of the three major features of Alzheimer disease (AD) tauopathy (neurofibrillary tangles, neuritic plaques, and neuropil threads) to the PET signal is incompletely understood, especially in the rare, dominantly inherited form of Alzheimer disease (DIAD). Here we compared in vivo flortaucipir PET in individuals with typical late-onset AD and those with DIAD to the microscopy of tau burden in a separate postmortem cohort. We found that flortaucipir PET measures total tau burden in both typical AD and DIAD, which is composed predominantly of neurofibrillary tangle and neuropil thread burdens.

Myelin

Diffusion MRI and probablistic tractography are powerful tools for analyzing long-distance connections within the white matter of the brain, but technical limitations of these methods have become increasingly apparent. One such limitation is the tendency for tractography streamlines to terminate preferentially at gyral crowns versus sulcal banks and fundi, resulting in a folding-related bias in apparent connectivity not observed in tracer and histology studies. Here we characterize this gyral bias by comparing diffusion MRI fiber orientations with axonal trajectories estimated from myelin-stained histology sections. We found that in many sulcal banks and fundi, fibers crossing the gray/white boundary could be seen on histology, but diffusion MRI fiber estimates could not be generated, likely due to the weak diffusion signal in these areas.
