Research

Amyloid-β

11C-Pittsburgh compound-B (PiB) positron emission tomography (PET) can measure the amount of amyloid-β (Aβ) in the brain, but to determine the types of Aβ plaques present, we need to compare antemortem 11C-PiB PET with postmortem stereology of Aβ plaques. We studied individuals with either typical Alzheimer disease (AD) or a rare, dominantly inherited form of AD (DIAD) and found that 11C-PiB PET correlates with diffuse and cored/compact Aβ plaques in typical AD, and diffuse Aβ plaques in DIAD, and that 11C-PiB PET was not greater in DIAD versus typical AD, even though there were more Aβ plaques, likely because Aβ plaques contaminate the 11C-PiB PET reference region in DIAD. We believe this suggests that diffuse Aβ plaques may play a larger role in DIAD than typical AD, and that DIAD and typical AD have fundamental differences in Aβ plaque pathology, so assumptions made when studying typical AD may not hold when studying DIAD.

Tau

18F-flortaucipir positron emission tomography (PET) can measure the amount of tau pathology in the brain, but to determine the types of tau pathology present, we need to compare antemortem 18F-flortaucipir PET with postmortem stereology of tau pathology. We studied individuals with either typical Alzheimer disease (AD) or a rare, dominantly inherited form of AD (DIAD) and found that tau pathology was composed predominantly of neurofibrillary tangles and neuropil threads in both DIAD and typical AD, but that both 18F-flortaucipir PET and tau pathology were greater in DIAD versus typical AD. We believe this suggests that neuropil threads are as important to investigate as their better-known counterpart, and that DIAD and typical AD have fundamental differences in tau pathology, so assumptions made when studying typical AD may not hold when studying DIAD.

  • 2020

Myelin

Probabilistic tractography uses diffusion MRI fiber orientations to estimate the structure of long-distance connections in the brain, but it tends to estimate that many more connections terminate in gyral crowns versus sulcal banks and fundi. To determine the validity of these estimates, we compared diffusion MRI fiber orientations with axonal fiber trajectories estimated from computer vision algorithms applied to myelin-stained histology slides. We found that axonal fibers terminating in sulcal banks and fundi were less heavily myelinated than those in gyral crowns, contributing to weaker diffusion MRI signals and fewer tractography terminations in these areas. We developed new algorithmic approaches to correct for this "gyral bias" in probabilistic tractography.
