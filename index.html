Graduate research

St. Louis, Missouri | 2018-2023

In graduate school, I co-founded the Pathology And Imaging Neuroinflammation and Tau (PAINT) committee to coordinate imaging-pathology studies between the Imaging and Neuropathology Cores of the Knight ADRC. I also joined the Dominantly Inherited Alzheimer Network (DIAN) to study autosomal dominant Alzheimer disease. Together, these efforts resulted in several studies comparing antemortem PET imaging with postmortem neuropathology in autosomal dominant versus sporadic Alzheimer disease.

I also led or was an active collaborator in several projects designed to determine the reliability of novel imaging and fluid-based biomarkers in diagnosing Alzheimer disease and monitoring the treatment effects of anti-amyloid-β therapies.

Post-baccalaurate research

St. Louis, Missouri | 2015-2018

In college I wanted to study brain function, but never got the chance. After college, I had the opportunity to study brain function in the context of learning risks and rewards in an uncertain environment. I thought that studying how biological neurons encoded risks and rewards could inform the design of artificial neural networks for reinforcement learning. At the time, there was a breakthrough in using neural networks to learn how to play Atari games without human supervision by using "only the pixels and the game score as inputs". However, when watching videos of how the neural network learned to play, I noticed it failed to score any points on the game "Montezuma's Revenge" and thus failed to learn anything at all. I thought that one way to remedy this was to add a "reward" signal for exploring the game environment to plan for the future instead of relying on the final game score as the sole reward. With this in mind, I ran experiments to measure a monkey's neuronal activity while it watched visual cues associated with different probabilities of future rewards. I ended up characterizing two relevant neuronal signals: one was associated with anticipating an uncertain reward (pre-reward ramping) and the other was associated with receiving a low-probability reward (post-reward spiking).

Afterwards, I had the opportunity to return to studying brain structure, but in a different context. Previously, I evaluated diffusion MRI estimates of axonal fiber directions against microscopy. Now, I developed a pipeline to evaluate diffusion MRI estimates of cellularity against microscopy estimates of cell fraction.

Undergraduate research

St. Louis, Missouri | 2011-2015

In college I worked on developing a way to estimate axonal fiber directions and delineate cortical surfaces from brain tissue sections. I was given brain tissue, a microscope, and a stack of paper, and was asked to draw what I saw. This took a lot of time. I decided to digitize the tissue sections so I wouldn't have to spend so much time drawing everything I saw under the microscope. Then I manually annotated everything using Adobe Illustrator. This still took a lot of time, so I wrote a MATLAB script to estimate axonal fiber directions using structure tensors and delineate cortical surfaces using B-splines. When comparing these microscopy estimates with diffusion MRI estimates of axonal fiber directions and cortical surfaces, I confirmed that microscopy and diffusion MRI showed the greatest agreement in axonal fiber direction along the gyral crowns but the greatest discrepancy along the sulcal banks and fundi of the cortical surface. This work supported the idea that there was a "gyral bias" in diffusion MRI tractography and motivated the development of new tractography algorithms.
