Research

Amyloid-β

Pittsburgh Compound-B (PiB) positron emission tomography (PET) can measure the level of amyloid-β in the brain, but cannot identify the types of amyloid-β plaques present. We compared antemortem PiB PET with follow-up postmortem stereology of amyloid-β plaques in individuals with typical Alzheimer disease (AD) or with a rare, dominantly inherited form of AD (DIAD). We found that PiB PET correlates with diffuse and cored/compact amyloid-β plaques in typical AD, and diffuse amyloid-β plaques in DIAD. However, PiB PET measurements were not greater in DIAD versus typical AD, even though amyloid-β plaque burdens were greater, likely because amyloid-β plaques can contaminate the PiB PET reference region in DIAD.

  • 2021

Tau

Flortaucipir positron emission tomography (PET) can measure the level of tau pathology in the brain, but cannot identify the types of tau pathology present. We compared antemortem flortaucipir PET in individuals with typical Alzheimer disease (AD) and those with a rare, dominantly inherited form of AD (DIAD) with postmortem stereology of tau pathology in a separate cohort. We found that flortaucipir PET likely corresponds to total tau burdens in typical AD and DIAD, which were predominantly composed of neurofibrillary tangles and neuropil threads.

  • 2020

Myelin

Diffusion MRI and probablistic tractography can measure long-distance connections within the myelinated white matter of the brain, but there is a tendency for tractography streamlines to terminate preferentially at gyral crowns versus sulcal banks and fundi, resulting in a folding-related bias in apparent connectivity. We compared diffusion MRI fiber orientations with axonal trajectories estimated from myelin-stained histology. We found that in sulcal banks and fundi, fibers crossing the gray/white boundary were less heavily myelinated than at gyral crowns, resulting in less accurate diffusion MRI fiber estimates.
