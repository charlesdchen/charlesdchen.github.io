Hi, I'm Charlie

I'm a PhD candidate in Imaging Science at Washington University in St. Louis. Medical imaging techniques such as MRI and PET are powerful tools for studying the human brain. However, they cannot truly resolve microsopic tissue structures of interest. I design ways to compare imaging results with microscopy findings to better understand what MRI and PET are measuring in the brain.

Amyloid-β

Pittsburgh Compound-B (PiB) positron emission tomography (PET) is used for identifying the presence of amyloid-β in the brain. However, PiB PET cannot distinguish between diffuse and cored/compact plaques, which are two sources of amyloid-β in Alzheimer disease (AD). Here we compared in vivo PiB PET with follow-up stereology of amyloid-β plaques in individuals with typical AD and those with a rare dominantly inherited form of AD (DIAD). We found that the PiB PET mainly correlates with diffuse plaque burden in DIAD and with both diffuse and cored/compact plaque burdens in typical AD.

Tau

Flortaucipir positron emission tomography (PET) is used for identifying the presence of tau in the brain. However, flortaucipir PET cannot distinguish among neurofibrillary tangles, neuritic plaques, and neuropil threads, which are three sources of tau in Alzheimer disease (AD). Here we compared in vivo flortaucipir PET in individuals with typical AD and those with a rare dominantly inherited form of AD (DIAD) with stereology of tau burden in a separate postmortem cohort. We found that flortaucipir PET likely reflects total tau burden in both typical AD and DIAD, which is mainly composed of neurofibrillary tangle and neuropil thread burdens.

Myelin

Diffusion MRI and probablistic tractography are powerful tools for analyzing long-distance connections within the white matter of the brain. However, there is a tendency for tractography streamlines to terminate preferentially at gyral crowns versus sulcal banks and fundi, resulting in a folding-related bias in apparent connectivity not observed in tracer and histology studies. Here we characterize this gyral bias by comparing diffusion MRI fiber orientations with axonal trajectories estimated from myelin-stained histology sections. We found that in many sulcal banks and fundi, fibers crossing the gray/white boundary could be seen on histology, but diffusion MRI fiber estimates could not be generated, likely due to the weak diffusion signal in these areas.
